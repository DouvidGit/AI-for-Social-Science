# Everyone Lines Up There: Visible Queue Length and High School Students' Line Choice

## Abstract

In everyday queues, people often face a tension between saving time and following the crowd, yet crowded lines are commonly misread as simple irrational conformity. Existing research on herding, informational cascades, social proof, and queue psychology shows that social information matters, but few classroom-scale tools isolate visible queue length in an interactive waiting scenario. I built *Everyone Lines Up There*, a one-minute online queue-choice simulation in which high school students choose among three equivalent entrance lines, one visibly more crowded, and explain their choice. ## Methods

### Artifact

The study used an online interactive artifact titled *Everyone Lines Up There*, deployed as a web-based queue-choice simulation. After an initial consent screen, participants entered a one-minute campus event entrance scenario. The screen displayed three selectable lines, labeled A, B, and C, for the same entrance. In the implemented version, one line was visually crowded with 12 people, while the other two lines contained 3 and 4 people. The crowded line was randomly assigned to A, B, or C each time the scene was rendered. Participants selected the line they would join by clicking either the visual queue area or the corresponding line button, then confirmed their choice and moved to a short feedback form. The page recorded the selected line, the crowded line, the full displayed queue counts, whether the crowded line was selected, the time from scene onset to queue click, perceived most crowded line, written choice reason, written avoidance reason, device type, timestamp, page URL, and user agent. Records were submitted to a Supabase table when the submission succeeded; if submission failed, the page showed a failure message and retained the latest record locally in the browser.

### Design

The implemented design was a single-scenario randomized-position design. The independent variable was visible queue length: one line was visibly longer than the other two, while the entrance purpose, labels, interaction style, and basic visual treatment were kept equivalent. The crowded line's position was randomized across A, B, and C to reduce simple position bias. The primary dependent variable was whether participants selected the crowded line. Secondary dependent variables included selected line label, response time in seconds, whether participants correctly identified the most crowded line, and the content of their written explanations. The post-choice questionnaire contained two open-ended items and one forced-choice perception item: why the participant chose the selected line, whether there was any line they especially did not want to choose and why, and which line they remembered as having the most people. The main comparison metrics were crowded-line selection versus shorter-line selection, choice distribution across A/B/C, response time by choice type, perceived-crowding accuracy, and coded themes in the open-ended answers.

### Consent

Informed consent was implemented as the first screen of the web experience. The main content remained hidden until the participant clicked "同意并继续" ("Agree and continue"). The consent text stated in plain language that participation was voluntary, anonymous, and limited to a classroom research purpose. It also stated that participants could exit at any time by closing the page. The activity was presented as a non-clinical classroom research page, and the interface stated that it did not collect names, email addresses, contact information, or other direct identifiers. The consent state was stored only in the participant's current browser session so that refreshing the page would not repeatedly show the consent screen during the same session.
